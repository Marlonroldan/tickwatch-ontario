    Smaller than you think

    How big is a tick, really?

    Nymphs cause most Lyme disease cases — and they're about the size of a poppy seed. Slide to compare each life stage against a Canadian quarter.

    Nymphs are why ticks are dangerous. They're active May through July, infected with Lyme bacteria from their mouse hosts, and so small that most bites are never noticed. Always do a tick check after time outdoors.

    "A nymph tick is the size of a poppy seed. Most people who get Lyme disease never saw the tick that bit them."

    — Public Health Ontario

      If you've been bitten

      Six steps. Five minutes.

      Most tick bites don't cause illness — but acting fast and correctly matters. Here's exactly what to do.

      1

      Remove it

      Fine-tipped tweezers, grab close to skin, pull straight up steadily. No twisting, matches, or Vaseline.

      2

      Save & photograph

      Ziploc bag plus a clear photo on a light background with a coin for scale.

      3

      Submit to eTick

      Free expert identification within 24-48 hours — the species matters more than testing the tick.

      4

      Check PEP eligibility

      Blacklegged tick + attached ≥36h + endemic area? Ontario pharmacists can prescribe single-dose doxycycline.

      5

      Watch for 30 days

      Expanding rash, flu-like symptoms, joint pain, facial drooping — see a doctor immediately if any appear.

      6

      Know the red flags

      Chest pain, severe headache, palpitations = ER now. These can signal Lyme carditis or neurological Lyme.
