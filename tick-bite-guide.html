I found a tick. What do I do?

A calm, step-by-step guide for Ontario. Most tick bites do not cause illness — but acting quickly and correctly matters.

If you have any of these right now, go to the ER: chest pain, fainting, severe headache with stiff neck, sudden facial drooping, or trouble breathing. These can be signs of Lyme carditis or neurological Lyme disease.

1Remove the tick

Use fine-tipped tweezers (pointed, not the blunt eyebrow kind) or a tick-removal card. Speed matters — the longer a tick is attached, the higher the chance of disease transmission.

How to remove it

  1. Grasp the tick as close to your skin as possible, right at the mouthparts.
  2. Pull straight up with steady, even pressure. Don't twist, jerk, or yank.
  3. If the mouthparts break off and don't come out easily, leave them — your skin will expel them like a splinter. Retained mouthparts do not transmit Lyme.
  4. Wash the bite with soap and water, then disinfect with rubbing alcohol.
  5. Wash your hands.

✓ Do

  • Use fine-tipped tweezers
  • Pull straight up, steadily
  • Save the tick (see step 2)
  • Note the date and bite location

✗ Don't

  • Burn it with a match
  • Smother it with Vaseline or nail polish
  • Twist or yank
  • Crush it with your fingers
Why no Vaseline, matches, or nail polish? These methods stress the tick and make it regurgitate its stomach contents — including Lyme bacteria — into the bite. Health Canada, Public Health Ontario, and the CDC all warn against them.

2Save the tick and identify it

Knowing the species of tick is the single most useful piece of information for deciding what to do next. In Ontario, only the blacklegged tick (deer tick) transmits Lyme disease.

Common ticks in Ontario — know what bit you

How to save it

  • Drop the tick into a small Ziploc bag or screw-top container.
  • Take a clear, in-focus photo of its back on a light surface, with a coin for scale.
  • Label it: date found, location on your body, and geographic location of likely exposure (park, trail, backyard).
Submit your tick to eTick.ca →

eTick.ca is a free service run by Bishop's University. Trained acarologists will identify your tick from a photo, usually within 24–48 hours. They do not test for diseases — but species ID is what your doctor or pharmacist actually needs.

Why not test the tick for Lyme? Ontario's public health labs no longer routinely test removed ticks. A positive tick doesn't mean you were infected, and the result comes back too slowly to guide treatment. Decisions are made on species + attachment time + your symptoms, not on tick PCR.

3Should you get preventive antibiotics?

For high-risk bites, a single 200 mg dose of doxycycline taken within 72 hours can significantly reduce the risk of developing Lyme disease. This is called post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

You may be a candidate if all of these are true:

  1. The tick is identified as a blacklegged tick (Ixodes scapularis), adult or nymph.
  2. It was attached for 36 hours or more (look for engorgement — a tick that hasn't fed looks flat).
  3. You can start treatment within 72 hours of removing it.
  4. The bite happened in a Lyme-endemic area of Ontario (most of southern and eastern Ontario — see PHO's risk area map).
  5. You have no medical reason you can't take doxycycline.
In Ontario, pharmacists can prescribe this. Since January 2023, Ontario pharmacists can assess and prescribe single-dose doxycycline for Lyme PEP under the Minor Ailments Program — no doctor visit needed. This is often the fastest route. Walk into any pharmacy with your tick and ID.

Other prescribers: family doctor, nurse practitioner, walk-in clinic, or Telehealth Ontario (call 811).

4Watch for symptoms for 30 days

Mark today's date on a calendar. Symptoms of Lyme and other tick-borne illnesses can appear anywhere from 3 days to several weeks after a bite.

Day 3–30 — Most common

Expanding red rash at the bite site (erythema migrans). The classic "bullseye" pattern only appears in about 70–80% of cases — many rashes are solid red, oval, or in hidden places (scalp, groin, behind a knee). Any expanding rash at or near the bite is a red flag.

Day 3–30 — Flu-like

Fever, chills, fatigue, headache, muscle and joint aches, swollen lymph nodes — especially if it's the wrong season for flu.

Weeks later

Facial drooping (Bell's palsy), severe headache with stiff neck, shooting nerve pains, heart palpitations, lightheadedness.

Months later

Joint swelling and pain — most often in the knee.

The bullseye is a myth — sort of. About 20–30% of people with Lyme never get a bullseye rash. No rash does not mean no Lyme. If you have flu-like symptoms in the weeks after a tick bite, see a doctor.

5When to see a doctor

Go to the emergency room immediately if you have: chest pain or palpitations, fainting, severe headache with stiff neck, sudden facial drooping, or trouble breathing.

See a doctor within a day or two if:

  • An expanding red rash at or near the bite (with or without bullseye pattern)
  • Fever, chills, severe headache, or unusual fatigue in the 3–30 days after a bite
  • Joint swelling, especially the knee
  • You're pregnant and had a tick bite
  • The tick was a Lone Star tick (rare in Ontario, but rising — they can trigger a serious meat allergy called alpha-gal syndrome)

Diagnostic testing

Ontario uses a two-tier blood test (ELISA followed by Western Blot). Important caveats:

  • Antibodies take 2–6 weeks to develop. Testing in the first 2–4 weeks of illness often comes back falsely negative.
  • If you have a clear bullseye rash and known tick exposure, your doctor should treat you without waiting for blood test results.
  • Other tick-borne infections (anaplasmosis, babesiosis) need separate tests.

6If the tick was on a pet

Remove it the same way — fine-tipped tweezers, close to the skin, steady upward pull. Dogs can get Lyme disease, anaplasmosis, ehrlichiosis, and babesiosis.

Call your vet if your dog develops: lethargy, lameness (especially shifting from leg to leg), fever, loss of appetite, swollen joints, or reluctance to move in the weeks after a bite.

Ask your vet about year-round tick prevention — Ontario's tick season is now 9–10 months long, and most vets now recommend year-round coverage in the south.
